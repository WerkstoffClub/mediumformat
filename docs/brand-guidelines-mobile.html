01·About

Records, the way
they should
be sold.

Medium Format is an independent record shop in Jakarta. The name comes from film photography, where the medium format is the larger negative that holds more detail. It also points to what we sell: the LP, the 12-inch, the 7-inch and the CD.

One platform sits behind the shop, the website and the marketplaces, keeping stock, orders and music in sync. The identity matches it: black and white, one typeface, and a single mark drawn from a record seen side-on.

02·How the brand should feel

Plain, and
sure of itself.

The character is in the words we choose and the restraint we keep, not in decoration. Let the records lead.

Plain

Clear words, clean layouts, no clutter. If something can come out, take it out.

Confident

Say things once and mean them. No hype, no exclamation marks doing the work.

Quiet

The records are the interest. The design stays in the background and lets them lead.

Honest

Describe what is real: the pressing, the condition, the price. Nothing dressed up.

12·How we write

Talk like
the shop.

Clear, friendly and to the point. We work in Indonesian and English, prices in Rupiah first.

Do
  • Lead with the record and why it is worth hearing.
  • Be specific about the label, year and condition.
  • Keep it short. One good line is plenty.
Don’t
  • Oversell, or invent scarcity to push a sale.
  • Use jargon that shuts newcomers out.
Instead of“INSANE restock!! Don’t sleep on this heater, link in bio.”
We’d say“Back in stock: Khruangbin, Mordechai. A few copies on the wall, come and have a listen.”

13·Photography and imagery

Let the records
be the colour.

Pictures should feel real and unfussy: records, sleeves, the shop, hands going through a crate. Plain light and honest framing. The cover art brings the colour.

What to shoot

Records and sleeves, the shop and its shelves, the turntable, people listening. Real moments over staged ones.

How it should look

Clean and a little understated. Black and white for the shop; let the sleeves carry the colour.

A simple test: would it sit happily on a record sleeve or a gig poster? If it looks like a tech advert, take it back a step.
